# Ard Fia na Fearach (Tree Giant)

#canon

**Biome:** [[Verdant Expanse]]
**Tier:** Boss (optional)
**Behaviour type:** Hostile to player only

---

## Overview

The apex creature of the Verdant Expanse. A massive bipedal tree beast that roams the map slowly. Autotroph. Not hostile to any other creature but always hostile to the player. The protector of the Expanse — attacked by nothing, deferred to by everything.

## Ecology

Autotroph. Roams all habitats within the Verdant Expanse. When on screen with any other creature, they perform a bowing or calming animation (the way dogs lower their heads). It is the ecological sovereign — the living embodiment of the biome's hierarchy.

## Behaviour

Patrols slowly. Always hostile to the player. Can be avoided due to its slow movement speed. If shot more than three times in its weak point, the creature initiates a boss fight, trapping the player in a root arena and creating root platforms for gameplay purposes.

## Attacks & Abilities

Melee attack is a one-hit kill. During the boss fight, it attempts to close the gap while [[Fiafhreine (Moss Deer)]], [[Cu Dorchath (Rune Tiger)]], [[Duibhear (Stone Ram)]], and [[Dealgshiul (Thorn Ambusher)]] spawn indefinitely as adds.

## Weaknesses

Glowing crystal eye. Must be hit three times to trigger the boss fight (or avoided entirely). Extremely tanky everywhere except the weak point.

## Consequences

If killed, the entire ecosystem of the Verdant Expanse becomes permanently hostile to the player. All predators start prioritising the player rather than their usual targets. This is a permanent world-state change with no reversal.

## Visuals

Massive bipedal tree. Should tower over everything else in the biome. The crystal eye should be visible from a distance — a clear but tempting target.

## Audio

Low rumbling footsteps. Creaking wood. The ambient sound of the biome should shift subtly when the Tree Giant is nearby. Boss fight music should incorporate organic percussion.

## Design Notes

The permanent hostility consequence is a meaningful choice the game does not warn the player about. Killing the protector turns the paradise hostile. The loot should be good enough to tempt players, and the consequence severe enough that they regret it. This mirrors the game's broader theme: removing the thing that holds a system together does not free the system. It breaks it.
