# Project Goals

---

## Minimal Viable Product

The MVP is an engine-ready template built in Godot: a single planet with functional core systems (movement, combat, NPC interaction, scene transitions, dialogue triggers, audio hooks) that the team can populate with content. A skeleton that accepts dialogue, sound, art, and level data without requiring structural rework. Placeholder entities are acceptable, even without sprites — one person codes untextured enemies while another adds dialogue or sound. Everything that ships in v1 is built on top of this template.

The game does not have an agreed single selling point in the traditional sense. What the team consistently identifies is atmosphere, ethical questions and decisions, NPC interactions, dialogue, and character building. The game will sell on the feeling of being in a world that asks something of you — not on a single mechanic. This is precisely what the RPG Maker games we referenced achieve. The MVP should mirror that approach: keep the effort required to create a single entity or environment low, so the team can iterate on what matters.

## Art Style

The team converges on: low-resolution sprites with expressive silhouettes and strong colour identity, placed against richer (but still stylised) backgrounds. Atmosphere carried by post-processing effects rather than asset complexity.

**Reference points:** Hyper Light Drifter (low pixel count, enormous atmosphere through colour and lighting), Rain World (simple creatures, complex ecosystems), Hollow Knight (simple character art, painterly backgrounds). The contrast between character simplicity and environment richness is itself a style.

Kate's summary: simplistic interactive characters and objects, complex and detailed background art. The interactive layer should be fun and appealing; the background layer provides immersion.

## Design Priorities (Team Consensus)

Scored 0–10 by all five members. Averages below.

| Category | Score | Definition |
|----------|-------|------------|
| **Atmosphere** | 10/10 | Preprogrammed and non-procedural content. Music, colour, environmental storytelling. Unanimous. |
| **Dialogue** | 8/10 | NPC interactions. Complex characters who convey the world rather than recite lore. High impact, not high volume. |
| **Streamline** | 7/10 | Focus and identity. How specific the game's selling point is. Reference scale: Ubisoft (0) → Hollow Knight (5) → Ultrakill (7.5) → Undertale (10). |
| **Mechanics** | 7/10 | Movement, physics, combat. Simple mechanics used cleverly over complex mechanics. Fluid, clear signals, no trial-and-error pattern memorisation. |
| **Lore** | 6/10 | Background story. Not explicit text — implied through gameplay and environment. Experienced rather than read. No wikis. |
| **Marketing** | 5–7/10 | The game should be something the team is proud of. Not built to sell, but built well enough that it could. Small price, reasonable playtime, accessible difficulty. |
| **Expansive** | 5/10 | Amount of explorable space. Currently one planet with ~2 hours of playtime. Overall ambition is larger but scoped by reality. |
| **Art style** | 3/10 | Low priority as a standalone category. Well-chosen colour palettes serve the atmosphere score. Pixel art over high-fidelity rendering. |

## Key Team Agreements

- Dialogue should be accessible but not mandatory. Players who skip it should still understand what is happening. Players who engage should find depth.
- Lore is backstory, not text. The player should piece it together from playing, not from reading tooltips or codex entries.
- No pointless filler quests. Every NPC interaction should carry weight.
- The game should support an "explorer" mode alongside a harder difficulty.
- Motivation and consistent output are the primary team risks. Address through clear task distribution and short-term deliverables.
